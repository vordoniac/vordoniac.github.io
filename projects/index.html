= Triangle Nonsense =

One day I needed to do weird things with triangles. Alright so - I made a java file that takes three points in 3d space, interprets it as a triangle in 3d space, and splits this triangle into isosceles triangles, and outputs the points of those triangles. If you didn't know, any irregular triangle can be split into at least 4 isosceles triangles, and any right triangle can be split into at least 2. Of course, because that wasn't enough, I then made an AutoHotkey file and programmed it to be able to output the XYZ-translation, X-scaling, Y-scaling, X-axis rotation, Y-axis rotation, and Z-axis rotation values needed to map equilateral triangles to reconstruct a triangulated (have all it's faces made triangles) 3D model given the points of all of it's triangles by splitting each irregular triangle into isosceles triangles and mapping those equilateral triangles to them (because you can scale any equilateral triangle to any isosceles triangle if you have independent control of X and Y scaling). This means that if you have a 3D editor of some kind, and you are able to get an equilateral triangle and also be able to modify those specific values, you could reconstruct any 3D-model despite any limitations the 3D editor has. Really, every part of this is complicated and it scares me that this is what I find fun and do with my free time.

= Tetris Baloney =

First, you've got to understand that in the making of this, me and my friend had a time limit. We chose to make Tetris as our programming project for our Computer Science A class. We thought it would be of moderate challenge and fun to do.

Well... it was fun.

...

I mean, in the end we had a product that was functional, but I'd say that I ended up doing a whole lot more work than I would have ever thought Tetris would need; I guess I can't really speak for my friend on that though. But, to be fair to my workload , we did a lot more than most people do when making Tetris. For example, most of the time when people code Tetris they don't include wall kicks, they have the piece stick to the bottom the second it touches the bottom, they pick the pieces completely randomly instead of using a bag randomizer like in the real game, they don't include the points for T-spins or T-spin minis (professional Tetris moves that, while obscure, award their own points), sometimes they don't even include the hold feature, and many times the product is just the result of them watching some lame YouTube tutorial. Of course, the Tetris we made had none of those problems, and we just went off of the HardDrop wiki and played the online version a zillion times to figure out how to actually program the game correctly. Oh yeah, dying. In our version, death is less of a "Game Over" and more like a "Game You Really Can't Do Anything Anymore". Like, the Tetris pieces just spawn in each other and you can't really move them so you just have to accept the score you have at that point and close the game. Here's where the time limit we had kind of shows. The not working menu buttons. The bad looking graphics. The weird first time you use the "hold" box. The fact that all the pieces are flipped in the "hold" box and in the "next" box. Look, I know it's not perfect, but the last thing I ever want to do is go back and try to understand our minamally commented code. Also, everything is programmed in Processing because that was the "thing" in our CompSci class at the time. You know what I mean, I think.

= Grammar Overload =

All together, this took about 3 months of work, but considering I had not much time every day because of school, that's probably why it took so long. Still though, I'm very happy with it. Also because it hadn't been done before. Also because it was just fun. Yeah. [Fun Fact: There are 12 rules for commas. 7 of which have an exception. 2 of those exeptions have an exception. Good times.] [The maps were made in Coggle if you were wondering.]
